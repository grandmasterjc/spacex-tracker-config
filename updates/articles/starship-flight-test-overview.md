# The Starship Program: An Evolving Flight Test Campaign

Starship is the largest and most powerful rocket ever flown. Each flight test pushes closer to the vehicle's full operational capability — orbital launch, payload deployment, in-space refueling, and eventually crewed missions to the Moon and Mars.

## The Vehicle

Starship is a fully reusable two-stage system:

- **Super Heavy** — the first stage booster. 71 meters tall, 9 meters wide, powered by 33 Raptor 2 engines producing roughly 74 meganewtons of thrust at liftoff — nearly double the Saturn V.
- **Ship** — the second stage and spacecraft. 50 meters tall, also 9 meters wide, powered by 6 Raptors (3 sea-level and 3 vacuum-optimized).

Stacked, the fully integrated vehicle stands 121 meters tall.

## The Flight Test Philosophy

Unlike traditional aerospace programs, SpaceX flies early and iterates fast. Each Starship test has specific goals — some test structural integrity, others test staging, reentry, or landing. Failures are expected and, critically, *planned for*. Hardware is built assuming some vehicles won't return.

Every flight generates data that feeds directly into the next build. Raptor engine design, heat shield tiles, flap aerodynamics, and catch tower mechanisms have all evolved across test campaigns.

## Key Milestones to Watch

When you open a live Starship mission in this app, here's what each event means:

- **T+0** — Liftoff. All 33 Raptors ignite; vehicle clears the pad.
- **T+~1:10** — Max-Q. Maximum dynamic pressure on the airframe.
- **T+~2:45** — MECO + Hot staging. Booster engines cut; Ship ignites while still attached.
- **T+~3:00** — Booster separation and boostback burn.
- **T+~7:00** — Booster landing burn (catch attempt at Starbase tower, or splashdown).
- **T+~8:30** — Ship engine cutoff at orbital velocity.
- **T+~45:00** — Ship reentry begins. Plasma trail visible on live feed.
- **T+~65:00** — Ship landing burn and splashdown / catch.

## Why This Matters

Starship isn't just a bigger rocket — it's designed to bring launch costs down by an order of magnitude. If the reuse architecture works as planned, it fundamentally changes what's economically possible in space: large telescopes, lunar infrastructure, and eventually Mars.

Every flight test is a step toward that future. We'll be watching, and so will you.
