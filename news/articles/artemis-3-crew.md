# Meet the Artemis III crew — and the mission that just changed shape

## Lead

NASA has named the four astronauts who will fly Artemis III — and confirmed a mission profile that looks very different from the lunar landing once promised. Instead of touching down at the Moon's south pole, the 2027 flight will stay in low Earth orbit and rehearse the one maneuver the entire Artemis architecture depends on: docking Orion with a commercial lander.

## The crew

Commander **Randy Bresnik**, 58, is a former Marine fighter pilot and TOPGUN graduate with two previous spaceflights behind him. Alongside him: **Frank Rubio**, who holds the American single-flight endurance record after 371 consecutive days aboard the ISS, and **Andre Douglas**, a Coast Guard reserve officer selected in 2021 who will be making his first spaceflight after serving as Artemis II backup crew.

The fourth seat makes history of its own. **Luca Parmitano** becomes the first ESA astronaut ever assigned to an Artemis mission — a milestone for the European partnership that supplies Orion's service module on every flight. NASA astronaut Bob Hines will train as backup.

## Why the mission changed

Artemis III was long billed as the return to the lunar surface. The revised plan is more modest and, arguably, more honest about where the hardware stands: SLS will launch Orion to low Earth orbit, where the crew will perform rendezvous and docking tests with test versions of one — possibly both — of the commercial Human Landing Systems in development by SpaceX and Blue Origin.

The SpaceX vehicle will be a Starship V3 taken off the production line and fitted with a docking adapter. Nothing about it will be lunar-ready — and that is the point. Before anyone descends to the surface on a later mission, NASA needs proof that Orion and a lander can find each other, dock, and transfer crew in space. That demonstration has never been done.

## The pieces are moving

The hardware for the mission is coming together. The upper section of the SLS core stage, built by Boeing, was completed at NASA's Michoud Assembly Facility this spring and arrived at Kennedy Space Center by barge in late April. Orion's service module — Parmitano's own agency's contribution — continues assembly in Bremen.

A LEO rehearsal in 2027 also sets the tempo for what follows: a successful docking demonstration clears the path for the surface landing on a later Artemis flight, while any major slip would ripple through the entire lunar program.
