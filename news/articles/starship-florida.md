# Starship is coming to Florida — and it changes everything about the launch schedule

**Category:** Starship  
**Published:** July 3, 2026  
**Hero image:** starship-v3.jpg  
**Image credit:** SpaceX / Space.com  

## Lead

For four years, every Starship flight has begun in the same place: Starbase, Texas. That era is about to end. SpaceX's Florida Starship program is now visibly sprinting toward its first East Coast launch — and the infrastructure taking shape on the Space Coast suggests a scale of operations the launch industry has never seen.

## The pad that sent Apollo to the Moon

At Kennedy Space Center's historic Launch Complex 39A — the same pad that launched Apollo 11 — deluge system testing has begun. That matters: water deluge commissioning is one of the last major milestones before a pad is declared operational, and industry observers now estimate 39A is just months away from hosting its first Starship stack.

The regulatory path is already clear. In February 2026, the FAA concluded a 16-month environmental review and authorized up to 44 Starship launches and 88 landings per year from LC-39A alone.

## 146 launches a year

The Florida buildout goes far beyond one pad. In December 2025, SpaceX received approval to redevelop Space Launch Complex 37 — the former Delta IV pad at Cape Canaveral — for Starship, with two launch towers now cleared for construction and capacity for roughly 76 missions per year.

Add the two pads at Starbase, and total approved US Starship launch capacity reaches 146 missions per year. For perspective: that is more launches than the entire world conducted in a single year not long ago — from one rocket family.

## A factory to match

Launches need vehicles, and vehicles need a factory. At Roberts Road, just south of KSC, SpaceX's "Gigabay" is nearing completion: the structural steel is finished, the roof nearly done, and the smaller vehicle doors installed. Designed for at least 24 vehicle workstations — four times the capacity of a Mega Bay at Starbase — the facility is expected to become operational by the end of 2026.

The message is unmistakable: Florida is not a backup site. It is being built as the high-cadence heart of the Starship program.

## What it means for launch watchers

Starship from 39A will not just be another line on the schedule. East Coast launches mean new trajectories over the Atlantic, new booster landing zones, new sonic boom footprints — and for millions of people along the US East Coast, the first chance to see the world's largest rocket fly with their own eyes.

No official target date has been announced, and Starship schedules are famously fluid. But between the deluge tests at 39A, the towers rising at SLC-37, and the Gigabay nearing completion, the direction is clear: the world's biggest rocket is about to get a second home.
