# Why Starship Flight 13 aborted at T-0 — and what happens next

**Category:** Starship  
**Published:** July 17, 2026  
**Hero image:** starship-abort-pad.jpg  
**Image credit:** SpaceX via livestream  

## Lead

Starship Flight 13 came within about a second of leaving the pad on Thursday. Then, in the final moments of the ignition sequence, four of the Super Heavy booster's 33 Raptor engines failed to start — and the vehicle's automatic abort system halted the launch with the rocket still firmly anchored to the mount at Starbase.

## What happened in those last three seconds

Engine ignition on Super Heavy begins roughly three seconds before liftoff. On SpaceX's webcast, the engine status graphic told the story in real time: 29 engines lit, four never did. The flight computer, seeing too few engines running to guarantee a safe climb, immediately commanded the running engines to shut down and kept the hold-down in place.

That is the system working exactly as designed. A Super Heavy that lifts off with too few engines could lack the thrust to clear the pad safely — the abort logic exists precisely to make that call faster than any human could.

Elon Musk confirmed the diagnosis shortly after: "Some of the engines didn't start, triggering an automatic launch abort." Two Raptor engines will be replaced on the booster.

## A first for the program

This was the first time a full-scale Starship stack has aborted at the last second after ignition had already begun. Falcon 9 has performed similar post-ignition aborts over the years — they look dramatic, but they are a routine part of how modern rockets protect themselves.

The engines in question are Raptor 3, the newest generation of SpaceX's methane-fueled engine. Flight 13 is the second flight of the upgraded Starship Version 3, and engine behavior has been the program's central challenge: Flight 12 in May also encountered engine difficulties, and SpaceX had modified the startup sequence ahead of this flight specifically to make ignition more robust.

## What Flight 13 is trying to do

The mission profile makes this abort more than a routine scrub. Flight 13 is set to deploy the first Starlink V3 satellites on a suborbital trajectory — a key step toward Starship taking over Starlink deployment from Falcon 9. Every week of delay ripples into that transition.

## What happens next

Swapping two Raptors on the pad is work SpaceX has done before, and Musk put the most probable next attempt at early next week. The vehicle and pad came through the abort undamaged, so this is an engine fix, not a rebuild.
